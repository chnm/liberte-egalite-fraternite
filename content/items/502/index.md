In the spring of 1765, the regional conflict between the Breton <i>Parlement</i> and the King spilled over to a higher level when the <i>Parlement </i>of Paris took up the case of Breton parlementary ally La Chalotais and began issuing its own remonstrances defending the regional <i>Parlement’s</i> power (issued 3 March 1766), under the doctrine of the "union of classes"—which held that all the <i>Parlements</i> were allied in speaking for the "nation." Supporters of the crown strongly opposed this idea, which they saw as contrary to the principle that the King alone could speak for the kingdom. The animosities generated by this "affair" continued to cut across French politics in the 1770s, since the Duke d’Aguillon retained his hostility to the Parlementary magistrates when in 1771, Louis XV made him foreign minister in the Maupeou government. This excerpt from the Paris <i>Parlement</i> from 1766 raises fears and attacks monarchical government in an inflammatory way, though still avoiding direct blasts against the King.
